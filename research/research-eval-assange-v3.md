- Primary angle: The CIA's plot to assassinate Julian Assange wasn't stopped by morality or law — it was stopped by the agency's own institutional dysfunction. The same bureaucratic turf wars, legal self-preservation instincts, and congressional leaks that make the CIA dangerous also make it incompetent when the target has enough shields. The world's most powerful intelligence agency got so humiliated by one man's leak of its own hacking tools that it spiraled into fantasies of London shootouts and plane sabotage — then got talked down by its own lawyers, not because killing Assange was wrong, but because getting caught would be worse. And then the system got what it wanted anyway, through slower, quieter, legal mechanisms that set a precedent more dangerous than any bullet.
- Hook idea: Open on the most operationally insane detail: the CIA drew up plans to shoot out the tires of a Russian diplomatic plane on a London runway to prevent Assange's extraction — and asked the British if they'd be willing to have a gunfight on the streets of London to stop a van carrying him to the Russian embassy. Then pin it: this wasn't a rejected pitch from a bad spy movie. This was discussed at the National Security Council. Thirty former US officials confirmed it to Yahoo News. The question isn't whether the CIA wanted Julian Assange dead. It's why the most powerful intelligence agency on earth couldn't manage to do it — and what that failure reveals about how American power actually works when it's driven by humiliation instead of strategy. Then rewind to the origin: who Assange was, what he built, and the decade-long escalation ladder that made the CIA lose its mind.
